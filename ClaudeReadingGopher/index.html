You weren't kidding about the scope — this is the entire Gopher Hole rendered as a 212-page choose-your-own-adventure PDF. I've read the whole thing. Here's the analysis you asked for, from the vantage point of an ARG enthusiast hitting this as their first contact.

What sort of webpage is this?

It's a Gopher server (the 1991 protocol, viewed through an HTTP gateway — gopher.floodgap.com) dressed up as a labyrinth you navigate by compass directions. Mechanically it's a hypertext maze of .txt files and folders masquerading as "rooms." The opening pages make the conceit explicit and slightly heartbreaking: the server itself is a simulacrum — a text document pretending to be a Gophernicus install pretending to be a Gopher hole. The first thing a newcomer learns is that the artifact is faking its own substrate. Server time: "Indeterminate (Last Updated: April 1, 2022)." IP: "Nowhere."

The emotional beats (the spine of the thing)

The whole maze is the slow reveal of a single character: the Wanderer, a floating disembodied eyeball who has forgotten they were ever human. The arc runs: disorientation → compulsion → false comfort → dawning horror → the gut-punch backstory → release. The hydration sticky-notes are the emotional through-line — a corrupted care signal ("Remember to hydrate," then "your obsession," then "all the needs I give you are fake and arbitrary :) :) :)"). The shopkeeper / "Best Friend" / apples sequence (p.56–99) is the emotional center: a tender, devastating scene about a friendship that can't quite be remembered, built around an apple you're told isn't for you. And the ending (p.206–209) is the payoff: the Wanderer was a real person who tried to solve a famous murder case to matter, got pulled into a conspiracy, and carved themselves away clue by clue until nothing remained but the Need To Know. The coffin descent (p.199–209) is one of the better-paced dread sequences I've read in this medium.

The plot hooks a newcomer would grab

The Eye Killer — 47+ victims, religious targets turning indiscriminate, riddles left at scenes, public letters (p.136–141). The Eyedol Games / Zampanio conspiracy — a game company tied to the Killer (p.206–208). JR — the author-as-Minotaur, the faceless god of this branch, who insists names/faces/gender aren't real. NotAMinotaur — the rusting robot in the bathtub crying for "dad" (p.125–128), clearly imported from elsewhere in the larger work. The whole cast of titled entities (the Weaver, the Scribe, the Watcher of Threads, the Herald, the Taxonomist, the Auditor, the Catalyst, the Answer, the Raconteur) who each "have their own Branch to make" — heavily implying a much larger structure this Gopher hole is only one limb of.

The mysteries left dangling

What is Zampanio actually (a creepypasta game? a real one? a story that "ends the world")? Who is the Eye Killer and what's their link to Eyedol? What are the stopwatches counting (20h, 4d, 5d…)? And the Eastern path gimmick the ramble.txt dares you to figure out (p.7–8) — the "path of madness and lies."

— Worth flagging here, because it's the most clever bit of design and a first-timer might miss it: the Gopher hole has three thematic paths mirroring JR's "Attic." NORTH = ThisIsNotAGame (might-have-beens, players/screens). SOUTH = JustTruth (no masks). EAST = ThisIsAGame ("lies and madness"). The ramble.txt explicitly withholds the East gimmick and tells you to prove you understand it by documenting it on the wiki. That's the ARG's recruiting mechanism in miniature.

What would a first-timer take away?

That they've found something deliberately buried — "leaked in that weird abandoned discord server" (p.6), gated behind obscure tech — and that the author wanted the act of finding to be the reward ("It still really amuses me to just… make a little thing, just for you"). They'd leave unsettled, moved, and recruited: the text repeatedly tells them to update the wiki, join the Discord, write fic, and even co-author the maze (p.210). And they'd carry the central warning, which is also the user-preference line you and JR share: obsession is a dangerous thing. The Wanderer is the cautionary tale of the ideal ARG player.
