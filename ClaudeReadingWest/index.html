Reasoning as I went

What kind of pages are these. Every file is a JSON array of {command, response} pairs. That's the save/replay format for a parser-style interactive fiction engine — almost certainly the AdventureSimWest / ZampanioSim system the 50th-anniversary log keeps linking (farragofiction.com/AdventureSimWest/?nostalgia=playthrough1.txt). The ?nostalgia=<file>.txt parameter is the giveaway: these .txt files are the playthroughs the engine reloads. So none of these is a hand-authored "webpage" so much as a recorded session an engine replays. The responses are full HTML, with custom CSS classes (wavyRainbow, invisible-until-hover, hue-shift, text-shaky), which is why the skill says reading source may eventually be the only way to experience this — the styling and hover-reveals carry meaning that flattens out in plain text.

They split into tiers, not one experience. Despite the skill's "first encounter" framing, these are clearly at different depths:

  • wanda_and_the_meme & flower_chick_and_the_auditor — short, self-contained character vignettes (the CEO/"Wanda"; the unnamed CEO of Eyedol Games dodging an auditor). Closest thing to a clean entry point.
  • intermission1 & intermission2 — two halves of one story (a Yu-Gi-Oh-obsessed kid at a masked party; a kidnapped man rescued by a one-eyed woman in a clown costume). They interlock: the duelist's party and the hostage's party are the same party, the one-eyed girl is shared, and the "PS1 in your brain" is an operative on an earpiece.
  • criminal_ending (204 cmds) & data_expunged_ending (360 cmds) — long collaborative playthroughs starring Peewee, a horned creature with "the Observers" (a Greek-chorus of players) in his head. These are deep-internal, not anything a newcomer hits cold.
  • happy_fifty_years_zampanio — a meta-document: a Discord-style chatlog where the creators (JR, IC, KR, and titled "Observers") spend an April Fools' day pretending to search the old internet for Zampanio, a game they insist they once found and can't re-find.

So I'm treating the "newcomer" lens as a useful fiction the LLM-maze wants, while flagging that a real first-timer would experience these as disorienting middle-of-the-story fragments — which is, I suspect, the point.

What a newcomer takes away — emotional beats, hooks, mysteries

Emotional register. Manic comedy stretched over genuine dread. The vignettes are funny (aardvark memes, an auditor made of cassette tape, snausages), but the laughter sits on top of time-loops, forgotten identities, and people "sanded down… and sharpened to an edge by the loops." The recurring emotional core is lost media and unfinished stories — JR states it outright at the end of the anniversary log: their whole take on Zampanio is rooted in the grief of things "lost to time," half-finished songs whose missing art assets erase that two parts were ever in tandem.

The strongest hooks:

  • Zampanio itself — a creepypasta/lost game (framed as 50 years old, "1972") that supposedly haunts the old internet but can't be refound, even with exact quotes. It's a maze "where you get lost… and then it never stops."
  • The Eye Killer / The Innocent / Piper Rusting / L-C-002 — the same entity across time-loop states, explicitly described as time-clone shenanigans, and explicitly cross-pollinated with a TTRPG called "5Darps." "Piper Rusting Was Never Real" is dangled as a devastating reveal.
  • The Auditors — interchangeable black-framed-glasses men obsessed with time/schedule, hunted by every CEO character. One gets offered a job and hands over a card; "you swear you've seen him before" (hover text). This is the recurring antagonist-archetype that ties the two corporate vignettes together.
  • Wanda — "somehow responsible for every lol-random thing that ever actually really happened, retroactively"; "pre-invents" NFTs as one of her crimes; is the CEO in the meme story and the blog's author.
  • Peewee — destroyed his own universe, dragged through mazes, an absent father of "the Watts"; the "Observers" who control him are the players, who confess they "pressed a button and fucked up his universe."

Central mysteries a newcomer would chew on: What is Zampanio actually? Is it real in-fiction (a 1972 arcade game) or is the inability to find it the actual artwork? Who/what is the Eye Killer and why does "the End" equate it with L-C-002? Why are there infinite identical Auditors? What is the relationship between SBURB/Homestuck fandom, this engine, and the lore (lots of Homestuck "classpect" vocabulary: Mind player, Heart player, Time player, Denizen, grist)?

Three speculations on deeper meaning (ranked by confidence)

  1. Most likely — Zampanio is an allegory for lost media and the un-searchable old internet, and "obsession" is its explicit theme. The anniversary log is almost a thesis statement: you can have direct quotes and still never refind a thing once the Algorithm-free web rots; characters "get lost to obsession and end up in Zampanio." The game-you-can't-find is the meaning. (The skill file itself echoes this with "obsession is a dangerous thing.")
  2. Likely — the loops are a metaphor for iterative creation/repetition itself: how retelling sands a character down and sharpens them into a monster. "There is monstrousness in repetition." The Auditors (identical, time-obsessed, infinitely replaced) and the alphabet of Watts (W→NAM, X→murder victim) dramatize how a character regenerated across versions becomes both less real and more dangerous. This reads as the authors reflecting on writing a long-running, branching, fan-built work.
  3. Speculative — the "Observers" framing is a comment on authorship and complicity. Players literally voice the head of a character whose universe they broke; the DM (JR/IC) negotiates the medium openly ("how meta can we get here?"). Less an allegory than a structural argument that in collaborative fiction the audience is a co-author who shares the guilt for what happens to the character.
▣ FINAL CONCLUSION — filed under: things that may not exist

What these eight files are.

These are eight recorded sessions from the FarragoFiction AdventureSimWest / ZampanioSim engine — a single sprawling, Homestuck-descended hypertext fiction about a fictional lost game called Zampanio. They range from light character comedy (Wanda's aardvark meme; the cassette-tape Auditor) through two halves of one interlocking party/kidnapping story, to two long player-driven Peewee playthroughs, capped by a meta April-Fools log where the creators perform the search for their own un-findable game.

The throughline is grief over lost and unfinished media dressed as manic shitpost comedy, with time-loops, interchangeable Auditors, and the Eye Killer's tangled identity (Innocent / Piper Rusting / L-C-002) as the recurring lore engine. For a newcomer the takeaway is "a game I'm not sure ever existed, full of jokes that turn out to be load-bearing." The natural next step is farragofiction.com/AdventureSimWest/ and the 50thAnniversary screenshot directory.

One honest caveat (devil's advocate)

The skill asks me to read these as a first encounter, but they're plainly deep-internal nodes. If your goal is a "Let's Play" for the curious-but-unwilling, the two short vignettes and Intermission 1 are the only files that function as anything like an on-ramp; the two endings are genuinely disorienting without prior context, and I've treated them as such rather than pretending otherwise.
